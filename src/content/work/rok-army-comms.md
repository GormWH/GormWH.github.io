An ROK Army artillery company can't return fire until its comms grid is connected, and for eighteen months that grid was my job — first as the signaller wiring it, then as the squad leader of the people who did. I was assigned to the company in August 2019 after intake and stayed until discharge in February 2021. The role had two layers, and the eight months I spent as squad leader were the second.

## Why the wire was the job

At every firing site the same chain had to come up first: FDC → gun → observer. The Fire Direction Center sends the firing solution, the gun fires, the observer corrects. Until the chain is wired, the battery can't return fire — and a battery that can't return fire is a battery that gets fired on first. That was the working stake, and it set the pace of everything we did on the ground.

The craft was unglamorous. I learned to strip the rubber sheath of a field wire with a single nipper, instead of carrying one tool to score the sheath and another to peel it. Two specialised tools work cleanly on a workbench; in the dark, on uneven ground, with the gun crews already in position, one tool that does both jobs is the discipline. Fewer items on the belt, one motion, fewer dropped tools. The scope of the job was wider than wire — wired and wireless, intra-company and inter-company links, plus the cryptographic side. The day's password keys travelled on a cord around the neck, and the password itself was delivered by mouth, soldier to soldier, every morning.

The closest the role came to a measurable peak was a battalion-wide signaller decryption competition. Three companies competed. We won on speed and accuracy.

## What the squad noticed

I was squad leader for eight of those eighteen months. The operating rule I tried to hold was simple to state and harder to keep: labor distributed equally regardless of rank, responsibility separated by rank, mutual respect as a working constraint rather than a virtue claim. If a wire run needed laying, the squad laid it together; if a call needed making to the platoon, it was mine.

What the rest of the battalion noticed was the absence of a small thing. Junior soldiers across most squads were visibly tense around their superiors — flinching at footsteps, double-checking before speaking. Mine were not.

ROK Army bonds usually dissolve at the gate. Five years on, the squad members I led are still in regular contact — we meet in person from time to time, and the working dynamic has held: mutual respect, no hierarchy, just friends.

## Beyond the squad

The unit threw me into close quarters with people I would not have crossed in civilian life. One had finished high school in New Zealand and university in Singapore, then worked as a fullstack engineer before service. Another had lived in Malaysia and Australia, graduated from a university in Australia, and worked as a hotelier. On the outside, the chance of these friendships forming would have been slim — our backgrounds are that different. 10+ months in close quarters put their views into mine anyway. The pivot toward software is one of the visible results.
